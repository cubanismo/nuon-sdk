Slide Show App -- overview

The slide show app is offered as a customizable tool for producing slide shows based on JPEG images and, optionally, MIDI music files. The sample can be built as is, and will demonstrate such a slide show. Of course, what's interesting is the possibility of substituting your own image and music files, and the documentation strives to clearly explain the steps for doing just that.

A good place to start is in the doc folder, with the User's Guide. This will explain how the slide show behaves to a user -- starting in a fully-automatic playing mode, but allowing entry to a manual mode with controls for random access to slides or MIDI files, or sequential access (forward or backward) for each. One can restart the automatic slide show with the PLAY button, optionally with a new viewing-time per slide.

If this sounds intriguing, try building and running the sample slide show. cd to the build directory and type "gmake"; this will produce a NUON.CD file that gets copied to the release directory, which also contains the Table of Contents for this particular slide show. Burning the NUON.CD file onto the root directory of a blank CD-R will allow you to play the show on an N501 or later NUON DVD player.

Note: the loading process on the N501 takes about a minute, and toward the end of it, the screen display may be partially overwritten with "hash". This is harmless, and just means that the frame buffer, containing the on screen image, has been overwritten by the loaded application. In a few seconds you'll see the warning splash screen, and can hit ENTER on the remote to begin.

Actually building your own slide show will require careful reading of the Author's Guide in the doc folder, and reasonable comfort with command-line interactions -- though not much more than you needed to type "gmake" and build the sample slide show. Certainly you don't have to be a programmer; what's required is patience for following directions, and a willingness to ask for help if you get into difficulties.

Finally, enthusiastic programmers may wish to study the code in the build and utils directories and make improvements in the slide show's capabilities. Or, they may be inspired to put a GUI front end on the entire process, which could simplify the operation enormously.

Slide Show App -- confessions

We agree that building a slide show should be carried out through graphical operations, such as dragging JPEG and MIDI files into target windows, filling out a dialog box to select options, and firing off the whole operation with one final mouse-click on a "build slide show" button. In fact, one of the check-box options should include burning a blank CD-R with the finished NUON.CD file, assuming the hardware is there to support it. We hope to supply such a GUI front end in a subsequent release, though it's possible that someone out there will beat us to it with a much better job than we could do.

The Author's Guide gives a detailed discussion of coping with the possible need to squeeze the slide show into a 4.5MB NUON.CD file. This need seems quite ludicrous -- the machine will read that file from a 650MB CD-R and then ignore all the other real estate on the same disc! This is one of those "historical" (hysterical?) problems that will soon get fixed, but we didn't want to hold up release of the NUON SDK on that account.

How serious a limtitation is this?

The sample slide show, with 33 pictures and 5 MIDI selections, is well within the limits referred to; another slide show I made had twice as many images and nearly two-hours of MIDI accompaniment. A third slide show, without MIDI, had 103 pictures and could have handled more. All images referred to are at the "high resolution" setting of 480-lines; there is also a 240-line setting you can choose, in which the practical number of images goes up by about a factor of four.

So, you ought to be able to get something interesting up while waiting for a less restrictive version to arrive.

Lastly, it remains to acknowledge the bare-bones nature of the slide show app. It was thrown together in a few weeks and lacks amenities such as title screens, crossfades, thumbnails with navigation capability (as in DVD menus), and on-screen confirmation of keypad entry, particularly for entering numbers that are used for navigation and resetting the per-image viewing time.

Shortcomings aside, we hope that you can have some fun with this, as we ourselves have done.
